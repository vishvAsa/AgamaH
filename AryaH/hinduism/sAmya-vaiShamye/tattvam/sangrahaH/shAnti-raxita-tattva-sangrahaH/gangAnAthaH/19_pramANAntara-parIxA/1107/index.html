тАЬAs a matter of fact, the idea of the night (and the man eating at night) cannot be derived from the sentence speaking of the day (and the man not eating); because what is expressed by the words of the latter has no connection with eating at night. Nor is there any contradistinction whereby the latter could indicate the eating at night.тАФNor can a second (and totally different) meaning be attributed to the words (speaking of the day); because these are already taken up in expressing another idea.тАФFrom all this it follows that the idea in question (of eating at night) is derived from a totally different sentence present only in the mind of the person.тАФThus then, this sentence (in the mind), though it is not actually verbally expressed, is yet duly cognised,тАФand it has to be pointed out which one among the means of cognition, perception and the rest, it is which brings about the cognition of the said (unspoken) sentence.тАФAs the sentence is not actually spoken, it could not be perception; nor could it be inference, because the sentence has never been found to be concomitant with the other factor; and if, even when this relation (of concomitance) has never been perceived, the factor concerned were regarded as an inferential indicative,тАФthen the utterance of any one sentence might bring about the cognition of all sentences; because on the point of being unrelated, there would be no distinction between one sentence and another.тАЭтАФ(1593-1598)тАФ[┼Ъlokav─Бrtika-arth─Бpatti, 56-61.]

The answer to this is as follows:тАФ[see verses 1593-1598 above]
