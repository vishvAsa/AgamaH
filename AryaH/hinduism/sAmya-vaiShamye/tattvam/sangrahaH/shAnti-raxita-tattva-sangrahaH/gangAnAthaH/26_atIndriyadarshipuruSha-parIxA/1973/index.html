It might be argued thatтАФWhen, in regard to a person it is found that, on being scrutinised by all Means of Cognition, what he has said cannot be gainsaid,тАФthen that person alone can be regarded as omniscient,

The answer to this is as follows:тАФ[see verses 3151-3153 above]

There is a certain reasoning adduced by the Buddhists in support of the omniscience of Buddha;тАФin this formтАФтАШBuddha must be regarded as a person who directly knew the true nature of all things,тАФbecause he has taught things unheard of, uninferred and in conformity with the real state of things,тАФjust like the ordinary man who has seen water and talks about itтАЩ, This same reasoning is put forward by the Digambara Jainas for proving the omniscience of Jina.тАФSo that the matter remains as doubtful as before.тАФAgain, when the Jaina has put forward his reasoning in support of the omniscience of Jina,тАФthe Buddhists put forward objections against it,тАФin the formтАФтАШThe teachings of Jina, in regard to Sy─Бdv─Бda and other doctrines being impossible, he cannot be regarded as omniscientтАЩ; these same are urged by the Jainas when the Buddhist adduces his reasoning in support of the omniscience of Buddha,тАФthe JainaтАЩs counter-reasoning beingтАФ

In this way this becomes a case like that of Reflection and Counterreflection: When the reflected object is there, its reflection appears; in the same way when the Reasonings and Counter-reasonings have been put forth, reasonings and counter-reasonings to the contrary come forward.
